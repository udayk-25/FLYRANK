## 2. Curation Decisions & Rejection Notes (Discernment)

A key part of image curation is rejecting graphics that look generic or cheapen the brand. Below are two AI-generated concepts that were explicitly rejected during the design process:

### Rejected Concept 1: Stock-style Developer Portrait
* **Prompt Used**: *"Professional software developer sitting at a desk, typing on a modern laptop, smiling naturally, soft studio lighting, realistic portrait."*
* **Why Rejected**: The generated output looked like a generic corporate stock photo. The expression was overly polished, and the lighting felt artificial. In a developer portfolio, a stock-like AI face looks fake and immediately makes a hiring manager distrust the authenticity of the page. 
* **Final Choice**: Replaced with a **Real Photo** headshot. Even a simple, phone-captured professional photo beats a high-gloss AI representation because it establishes genuine human trust.

### Rejected Concept 2: Cyberpunk Neon Workspace
* **Prompt Used**: *"Futuristic computer workspace, neon blue and purple light beams, holographic code elements floating, dark tech background."*
* **Why Rejected**: The output was chaotic and visually busy. The bright purple and neon glows competed with the actual data charts and code snippets. A portfolio's design should frame the work, not overshadow it. The high-contrast neon violated the clean, quiet Slate/Sky identity kit we established in FL-04.
* **Final Choice**: Replaced with a **minimalist geometric pattern** with thin, clean sky-blue lines on slate (`hero_geometric_bg.png`), keeping the aesthetic quiet so the actual case studies stand out.

## 3. Credibility: Real vs. AI Stand-ins

For case studies and engineering proofs, **real captures must always beat AI-generated stand-ins**:

* **Why we do this**: A tech manager will spot a fake, AI-generated IDE screen or a mockup chart instantly. Using a generated graphic for a case study (e.g. showing a fake code editor with scrambled text) signals that the developer has nothing real to show.
* **Our Implementation**: We use real terminal logs and exact SVG charts generated by our Python scripts. When showing the Random Forest performance, we link directly to [top_feature_importance.svg](file:///c:/Users/Uday/Downloads/FLYRANK/outputs/charts/top_feature_importance.svg) — proving that the pipeline actually ran and produced measurable results.
